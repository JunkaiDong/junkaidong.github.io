Making Sense of Reshetikhin-Turaev Invariant: Uq(sl2)U_q(\mathfrak{sl}_2)Uq​(sl2​), Kauffman, and Jones

There has been several pieces of common wisdom that I have collected, but have not yet verified. For example, category theories are useful for producing topological invariants. This is a demonstration of this common wisdom, which was substantiated by Sunghyuk Park in his course on quantum topology in Fall 2024. Notes can be found at this link: this short example only uses the more elementary first half of the course. I warn readers that this note is only a execution of the operational procedure without going into deeper conceptual understandings. In fact, most of the content is a direct copy of the lecture notes, so please go and read them for a more thorough exposition. All of the figures are copied from the lecture notes as well. Mathematica code to reproduce all calculations performed in this note can be found here.

Reshetikhin-Turaev: topological invariant of knots

Reshetikhin and Turaev in their seminal paper [1] stated the following: given a ribbon category C\mathcal{C}C, one can produce invariants of knots (or more generally ribbon graphs.) A ribbon category needs the following data:

  1. Objects and morphisms that make C\mathcal{C}C a category.

  2. (Monoidal.) To make C\mathcal{C}C monoidal, we need (a) a tensor product structure ⊗:C×C→C\otimes: \mathcal{C}\times\mathcal{C}\to\mathcal{C}⊗:C×C→C and (b) an identity object I\mathbb{I}I s.t. I⊗A=A\mathbb{I}\otimes A = AI⊗A=A for all A∈ObCA\in\textrm{Ob}\mathcal{C}A∈ObC. (Furthermore, some diagrams needs to commute.)

  3. (Braided.) To make the monoidal category C\mathcal{C}C braided, consider the swap map τ:\tau:τ: C×C→C×C,(A,B)→(B,A),(f,g)→(g,f)\mathcal{C}\times\mathcal{C}\to\mathcal{C}\times\mathcal{C}, (A,B)\to (B,A), (f,g)\to(g,f)C×C→C×C,(A,B)→(B,A),(f,g)→(g,f). The opposite tensor product is defined as ⊗op=⊗∘τ\otimes^{\mathrm{op}}=\otimes\circ\tau⊗op=⊗∘τ. Then, the monoidal category C\mathcal{C}C is braided if there exists a family of isomorphisms βA,B:A⊗B→B⊗A\beta_{A,B}: A\otimes B\to B\otimes AβA,B​:A⊗B→B⊗A, and for all morphisms f:A→A′,g:B→B′f:A\to A', g:B\to B'f:A→A′,g:B→B′, (g⊗f)βA,B=βA′,B′(f⊗g)(g\otimes f)\beta_{A,B}=\beta_{A',B'}(f\otimes g)(g⊗f)βA,B​=βA′,B′​(f⊗g). (Furthermore, some diagrams needs to commute.)

  4. (Ribbon.) To make the braided category C\mathcal{C}C a ribbon category, one needs two concepts: one is the concept of duality and rigidity, and the other is the concept of twist. A monoidal category is right/left rigid if all objects AAA, there are right/left duals A∗A^*A∗ / ∗ ⁣ ⁣A^*\! \!A∗A. For left rigid categories, we have the right cup and cap morphisms: ∪⃗A:I→\vec{\cup}_A:\mathbb{I}\to∪A​:I→ ∗ ⁣ ⁣A⊗A^* \! \!A\otimes A∗A⊗A and ∩⃗A:A⊗ ∗ ⁣ ⁣A→I\vec{\cap}_A: A\otimes \ ^* \! \!A\to \mathbb{I}∩A​:A⊗ ∗A→I. They need to satisfy zigzag identities. There also needs to be a compatible twist θ\thetaθ, i.e. a natural family of isomorphisms θA:A→A\theta_A:A\to AθA​:A→A, for which θA⊗B=βB,AβA,B(θA⊗θB)\theta_{A\otimes B}=\beta_{B,A}\beta_{A,B}(\theta_A\otimes\theta_B)θA⊗B​=βB,A​βA,B​(θA​⊗θB​).

If all four items of the data are provided, the category C\mathcal{C}C is a ribbon category.

Ribbon Category from Hopf algebra data

These ribbon categories are still a bit abstract for me. What's great is that you can construct them from something a little bit more concrete called the Hopf algebra. The Hopf algebra first needs to be a bialgebra: i.e. both an algebra and a coalgebra. A bialgebra is a tuple (B,μ,η,Δ,ϵ)(B,\mu,\eta,\Delta,\epsilon)(B,μ,η,Δ,ϵ), where μ\muμ the product and ϵ\epsilonϵ the unit make BBB an algebra, and Δ\DeltaΔ the coproduct and η\etaη the counit make BBB a coalgebra, and furthermore Δ\DeltaΔ and ϵ\epsilonϵ are algebra morphisms. By coproduct, we mean a linear map B→B⊗BB\to B\otimes BB→B⊗B. It has to satisfy the dual of the associativity rule: (Δ⊗id)Δ=(id⊗Δ)Δ(\Delta\otimes \textrm{id})\Delta = (\textrm{id}\otimes\Delta)\Delta(Δ⊗id)Δ=(id⊗Δ)Δ. The counit ϵ:B→k\epsilon: B\to\mathbb{k}ϵ:B→k is such that (ϵ⊗id)Δ=id=(id⊗ϵ)Δ(\epsilon\otimes \textrm{id})\Delta=\textrm{id}=(\textrm{id}\otimes \epsilon)\Delta(ϵ⊗id)Δ=id=(id⊗ϵ)Δ.

A Hopf algebra is a bialgebra BBB such that the identity map is invertible in the convolution algebra End(B)\textrm{End}(B)End(B). In plain words, there exists an antipode S∈End(B)S\in\textrm{End}(B)S∈End(B) s.t.

μ∘(S⊗id)∘Δ=η∘ϵ.\mu\circ (S\otimes \textrm{id})\circ \Delta=\eta\circ\epsilon.μ∘(S⊗id)∘Δ=η∘ϵ.

Some quick examples to keep in mind: the group algebra k[G]\mathbb{k}[G]k[G] is a cocommutative Hopf algebra where

Δ(g)=g⊗g,ϵ(g)=1,S(g)=g−1.\Delta(g)=g\otimes g,\quad \epsilon(g)=1,\quad S(g)=g^{-1}.Δ(g)=g⊗g,ϵ(g)=1,S(g)=g−1.

The dual algebra is also a Hopf algebra if GGG is finite:

Δ(f)(g⊗h)=f(gh),ϵ(f)=f(e),S(f)(g)=f(g−1).\Delta(f)(g\otimes h)=f(gh),\quad \epsilon(f)=f(e),\quad S(f)(g) = f(g^{-1}).Δ(f)(g⊗h)=f(gh),ϵ(f)=f(e),S(f)(g)=f(g−1).

For Hopf algebra BBB, the category B−ModfinB-\textrm{Mod}^{\textrm{fin}}B−Modfin is naturally a rigid monoidal category. The coproduct Δ\DeltaΔ provides the action of HHH on tensor products, and SSS provides the action on the dual modules.

To make a Hopf algebra BBB braided, one equips it with an invertible element R∈B⊗BR\in B\otimes BR∈B⊗B known as the univeral RRR-matrix. It needs to satisfy a bunch of properties. Most importantly, it makes B−ModB-\textrm{Mod}B−Mod naturally a braided monoidal category, with the braid morphisms given by:

βV,W=σV,W(R(v⊗w)),\beta_{V,W}=\sigma_{V,W}(R(v\otimes w)),βV,W​=σV,W​(R(v⊗w)),

where σ\sigmaσ exchanges the objects.

Finally, let's suppose SSS the antipode is invertible. Then, we can consider the element

u=∑iS(ti)si,R=∑isi⊗ti.u=\sum_i S(t_i)s_i,\quad R=\sum_i s_i\otimes t_i.u=i∑​S(ti​)si​,R=i∑​si​⊗ti​.

One can prove that uS(u)uS(u)uS(u) is central. Its square root ν\nuν is called the ribbon element:

ν2=uS(u),\nu^2 = uS(u),ν2=uS(u),

and using it we can define the twist morphisms:

θV:V→V,v→ν−1v.\theta_{V}: V\to V, v\to\nu^{-1} v.θV​:V→V,v→ν−1v.

Concrete example: Uq(sl2)U_q(\mathfrak{sl}_2)Uq​(sl2​)

Uq(sl2)U_q(\mathfrak{sl}_2)Uq​(sl2​) is a quantum group. I do not know why it is called this way. Semantically, it seems that it can be interpreted as a "quantized" version of the Lie algebra sl2\mathfrak{sl}_2sl2​, which we will explain below.

Now, we try to prove the skein relation for Kauffman brackets. We first color the diagrams: we color the two ends at the bottom with V2V_2V2​ (i.e. arrows going in), and the two ends at the top also V2V_2V2​ (i.e. arrows going out). This means that for the diagram on the left we have βV2,V2=σV2,V2R\beta_{V_2,V_2}=\sigma_{V_2,V_2} RβV2​,V2​​=σV2​,V2​​R; on the right, the first diagram is the identity, but the second diagram is confusing. What should we do when two ingoing arrows meet?

Note that we only know what happens when one arrow goes in and one goes out; those are the cup and cap morphisms. This means that we need to flip the arrows for one of the top ends and one of the bottom ends. This can be done using the following Uq(sl2)−ModU_q(\mathfrak{sl}_2)-\textrm{Mod}Uq​(sl2​)−Mod isomorphism g:V2→V2∗g:V_2\to V_2^*g:V2​→V2∗​:

Thus, for example, the bottom portion where both arrows are going in can be computed as ∩⃗V2∘(id⊗g)\vec{\cap}_{V_2}\circ(\textrm{id}\otimes g)∩V2​​∘(id⊗g). From this, we can successfully verify the skein relation (up to a minus sign):

To compute the Jones polynomial of the unknot, one splits it into a left cap and a right cup. The composition of these morphisms will give q+q−1=[2]q+q^{-1}=[2]q+q−1=[2], which also agrees up to a minus sign. This thus proves that the RT invariant of links colored by V2V_2V2​ reproduces the Kauffman bracket.

The procedure to produce the Jones polynomials is a bit trickier. For the first diagram on the left of the skein relation one needs to use q−3/2βV2,V2=(θV2−1⊗id)βV2,V2q^{-3/2}\beta_{V_2,V_2}=(\theta_{V_2}^{-1}\otimes \textrm{id})\beta_{V_2,V_2}q−3/2βV2​,V2​​=(θV2​−1​⊗id)βV2​,V2​​, and for the second diagram one needs to use q3/2βV2,V2−1=(θV2⊗id)βV2,V2−1q^{3/2}\beta_{V_2,V_2}^{-1}=(\theta_{V_2}\otimes \textrm{id})\beta_{V_2,V_2}^{-1}q3/2βV2​,V2​−1​=(θV2​​⊗id)βV2​,V2​−1​. The reason for this is exactly the "covariance" under the R1 move, but I do not know how to mathematically justify this from the RT perspective. After all, the RT invariant is an invariant of ribbon diagrams, which cares about the twisting of ribbons, rather than knots.

Hall Conductance from Edges

We all know that in quantum Hall-like systems, edge states dominate the transport signatures. How do we see that?

Let us focus on the simplest states: the ∣ν∣=1/m|\nu|=1/m∣ν∣=1/m Laughlin states. The transport signatures are:

G2T=∣GH4T∣=e2mh,G^{2T} = |G_H^{4T}| = \frac{e^2}{mh},G2T=∣GH4T​∣=mhe2​,

in which G=I/VG=I/VG=I/V is the conductance. For two terminal we employ the following geometry:

Two terminal conductance: bottom edge

The two terminal conductance calculation is easy to perform, and we can actually derive the four terminal conductance from the two terminal conductance. We begin the calculation by writing down the edge theory for the bottom edge. The edge theory of such states is a simple chiral Luttinger liquid (note that here I am writing down the Minkowski action!):

S=m4π∫dxdt ∂xϕ(∂t+v∂x)ϕS = \frac{m}{4\pi}\int dxdt \ \partial_x\phi(\partial_t+v\partial_x)\phiS=4πm​∫dxdt ∂x​ϕ(∂t​+v∂x​)ϕ

The dictionary between ϕ\phiϕ and physical quantities is:

ρ(x)=e2π∂xϕ,j(x)=e2π∂tϕ.\rho(x) = \frac{e}{2\pi}\partial_x \phi,\quad j(x) = \frac{e}{2\pi}\partial_t \phi.ρ(x)=2πe​∂x​ϕ,j(x)=2πe​∂t​ϕ.

In this sense, the second term v(∂xϕ)2=vρ2v(\partial_x\phi)^2=v\rho^2v(∂x​ϕ)2=vρ2 corresponds to the interaction term, which we are familar with in the language of bosonization. We can now calculate the response along the edge based on linear response:

π(x)=δSδ(∂tϕ)=m2π∂xϕ(x),\pi(x) = \frac{\delta S}{\delta(\partial_t \phi)} = \frac{m}{2\pi}\partial_x\phi(x),π(x)=δ(∂t​ϕ)δS​=2πm​∂x​ϕ(x),

Assuming that ϕ(x,t)=∫dq2πϕqei(qx−ω(q)t),\phi(x,t) = \int \frac{dq}{2\pi }\phi_q e^{i(qx-\omega(q)t)},ϕ(x,t)=∫2πdq​ϕq​ei(qx−ω(q)t), we obtain

Two terminal conductance: both edges

In the above calculation, we have performed the calculation for one edge only. In reality, there is both a top and a bottom edge. Performing the same calculation for the top edge gives us a contribution of jt(0)=−e2mhV(∞)j_t(0) = -\frac{e^2}{mh}V(\infty)jt​(0)=−mhe2​V(∞). Assuming that the edges run parallel, we simply add up their current contributions. Further denoting VL=V(−∞),VR=V(∞)V_L=V(-\infty), V_R=V(\infty)VL​=V(−∞),VR​=V(∞), we obtain

j(0)=jb(0)+jt(0)=e2mh(VL−VR)j(0) = j_b(0)+j_t(0) = \frac{e^2}{mh}(V_L-V_R)j(0)=jb​(0)+jt​(0)=mhe2​(VL​−VR​)

which means that

G2T=e2mhG^{2T} =\frac{e^2}{mh}G2T=mhe2​

the expected value.

Four terminal conductance: Landauer-Buttiker

To compute the four terminal conductance one needs to study the geometry a bit better:

The measurement is performed such that there are four leads, namely top, right, bottom, and left (i=1i=1i=1 through 444). A current III is passed through the left lead to the right lead. Maintaining the current through the top and bottom leads to be 000, we measure the voltage difference VtbV_{tb}Vtb​. The four terminal conductance is computed as

GH4T=IVtb.G_{H}^{4T} = \frac{I}{V_{tb}}.GH4T​=Vtb​I​.

Given that transport along the edges are ballistic, one can employ the Landauer-Buttiker formalism to compute the conductance:

Ii=∑jGijVj I_i = \sum_{j} G_{ij} V_j Ii​=j∑​Gij​Vj​

in which IiI_iIi​ is the current flowing into lead III, GijG_{ij}Gij​ is the ballistic conductance from lead jjj to lead iii, and VjV_jVj​ is the voltage of lead jjj. Given the geometry above, the conductance matrix elements can be written as

Gij=G2Tδi+1,j, G_{ij}=G^{2T}\delta_{i+1,j}, Gij​=G2Tδi+1,j​,

where the delta function is understood mod 444. Given the current vector I=(0,−I,0,I)I=(0,-I,0,I)I=(0,−I,0,I), one can solve that GH4T=G2T.G_{H}^{4T} = G^{2T}.GH4T​=G2T.

The paradox: wrong conductance

This fantastic scenario is immediately destroyed when there are anti-chiral edge modes. For concreteness, let us imagine the ν=2/3\nu=2/3ν=2/3 quantum Hall state, which one can naively imagine to be a ν=1\nu=1ν=1 state with a charge eee chiral edge mode stacked with a ν=−1/3\nu=-1/3ν=−1/3 state (my understanding: charge eee electrons in a 1/31/31/3 Laughlin state with the opposite magnetic field) with a charge e/3e/3e/3 anti-chiral edge mode. We can understand it simply by looking at the two-terminal conductance with the following geometry:

The two edge modes have gR=e2/hg_R = e^2/hgR​=e2/h and gL=e2/3hg_L=e^2/3hgL​=e2/3h here. Although we expect the correct conductance to be G2T=gL−gR=2e2/3hG^{2T}=g_L-g_R = 2e^2/3hG2T=gL​−gR​=2e2/3h, a simple calculation shows that this is not the case. Indeed, for the bottom edge we have

jb=gRVL−gLVR,j_b = g_RV_L - g_LV_R,jb​=gR​VL​−gL​VR​,

and for the top edge we have

jt=gLVL−gRVR.j_t = g_LV_L - g_RV_R.jt​=gL​VL​−gR​VR​.

Summing up, we obtain

G2H=gL+gR!G^{2H} = g_L+g_R!G2H=gL​+gR​!

One can also check that the four terminal Hall conductance is also not correct from the following geometry:

We employ the Landauer-Buttiker formalism, this time with the conductance matrix

Gij=gRδi+1,j+gLδi−1,j, G_{ij}=g_R\delta_{i+1,j}+g_L\delta_{i-1, j}, Gij​=gR​δi+1,j​+gL​δi−1,j​,

where again the delta functions are understood mod 444. From this, we can solve for the four terminal conductance to be

GH4T=gR2+gL2gR−gL. G_H^{4T} = \frac{g_R^2+g_L^2}{g_R-g_L}. GH4T​=gR​−gL​gR2​+gL2​​.

Thus, this formula can only be correct if the edge is perfectly chiral!

This paradox was first observed by Kane, Fisher and Polchinski in Ref. [1]. Below we discuss their solution to this paradox.

Disorder at play

What the authors realized is that the edge is unfortunately disordered. Disorder acts like a local potential and can introduce scattering from one edge mode into another edge mode. Particularly, they write down the following (Minkowski) action for the bottom edge:

S=S0+S1 S = S_{0}+S_1 S=S0​+S1​

where

S0=−14π∫dxdt [∂xϕ1(∂t+v1∂x)ϕ1+3∂xϕ2(−∂t+v2∂x)ϕ2+2v12∂xϕ1∂xϕ2] S_{0} = -\frac{1}{4\pi}\int dxdt \ [ \partial_x\phi_1(\partial_t+v_1\partial_x)\phi_1 +3\partial_x\phi_2(-\partial_t+v_2\partial_x)\phi_2+2v_{12}\partial_x\phi_1\partial_x\phi_2] S0​=−4π1​∫dxdt [∂x​ϕ1​(∂t​+v1​∂x​)ϕ1​+3∂x​ϕ2​(−∂t​+v2​∂x​)ϕ2​+2v12​∂x​ϕ1​∂x​ϕ2​]

and

S1=∫dxdt ξ(x)ei(ϕ1+3ϕ2)+c.c. S_1 = \int dx dt \ \xi(x) e^{i(\phi_1+3\phi_2)}+c.c. S1​=∫dxdt ξ(x)ei(ϕ1​+3ϕ2​)+c.c.

Again, we remind ourselves of the dictionary:

ρ(x)=e2π∂x(ϕ1+ϕ2),j(x)=e2π∂t(ϕ1+ϕ2). \rho(x) = \frac{e}{2\pi}\partial_x(\phi_1+\phi_2), \quad j(x) = \frac{e}{2\pi}\partial_t(\phi_1+\phi_2). ρ(x)=2πe​∂x​(ϕ1​+ϕ2​),j(x)=2πe​∂t​(ϕ1​+ϕ2​).

Furthermore, eiϕ1e^{i\phi_1}eiϕ1​ creates a charge eee particle in mode ϕ1\phi_1ϕ1​, whereas e−iϕ2e^{-i\phi_2}e−iϕ2​ creates a charge e/3e/3e/3 particle in mode ϕ2\phi_2ϕ2​. (This can be seen from the commutation relations of [∂xϕi,ϕi][\partial_x\phi_i, \phi_i][∂x​ϕi​,ϕi​] and computing the charge density created by e±iϕie^{\pm i \phi_i}e±iϕi​.) Thus, indeed S1S_1S1​ describes a disorder potential induced process that tunnels 333 e/3e/3e/3 particles from ϕ2\phi_2ϕ2​ to an eee particle in ϕ1\phi_1ϕ1​. ξ(x)\xi(x)ξ(x) describes a Gaussian random variable with short range correlations:

Indeed if we choose to use the Luttinger liquid action S0S_0S0​ alone, we would have obtained

where c=2v12/3(v1+v2)c=2v_{12}/\sqrt{3}(v_1+v_2)c=2v12​/3​(v1​+v2​). The interactions v12v_{12}v12​ strongly modify both conductances – their robustness in experimental measurements thus suggest that taking S0S_0S0​ only is not the best choice.

How important is the disorder?

The disorder-dominated phase

One might think that the strongly coupled phase is not solvable. This is shown to not be the case. We first define new variables

A couple comments are in order, which are kindly pointed out by Patrick Ledwith and Eslam Khalaf. Firstly, although the disorder term is shown to be perturbatively relevant for Δ<3/2\Delta<3/2Δ<3/2, it is unclear where the IR fixed point will be. The above calculation shows that the Δ=1\Delta=1Δ=1 point is perturbatively stable, but it does not establish that for any Δ<3/2\Delta<3/2Δ<3/2 the theory will flow to Δ=1\Delta=1Δ=1. Secondly, although a rewriting of SSS in terms of ϕρ\phi_\rhoϕρ​ and ϕσ\phi_\sigmaϕσ​ is possible in the clean limit, one would find that vvv is marginal and would not flow. This is why disorder induced correlations are important. Thirdly, a more careful analysis of the random couplings between ϕρ\phi_\rhoϕρ​ and ψ~\tilde\psiψ~​ based on replica tricks can be found in [2].

Experimental signature

To summarize: strong disorder reconstructs the edge structure of the ν=2/3\nu=2/3ν=2/3 state. It transforms the 1−1/31-1/31−1/3 edge, i.e. a chiral charge eee mode and an anti-chiral charge e/3e/3e/3 mode, to a 2/3+02/3+02/3+0 edge, i.e. a chiral charge 2e/32e/32e/3 mode and an anti-chiral charge neutral mode.

Aside from the tunneling experiments, thermal measurements is enough to probe the existence of the neutral mode. Although the neutral mode cannot carry electric charge, it still carries thermal energy. Given that the neutral mode will equilibrate when it goes into the source lead, there will be heat noise near the source lead due to the neutral mode. The charged mode will create electric and thermal noise near the drain lead. This has been measured by Ref. [3].

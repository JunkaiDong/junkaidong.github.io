Kramers-Wannier Duality

Consider 1D Transverse Field Ising Model:

H=−∑iσizσi+1z−g∑iσixH=-\sum_i\sigma^z_i\sigma^z_{i+1}-g\sum_i\sigma^x_iH=−i∑​σiz​σi+1z​−gi∑​σix​

It is well known by K-W that H(g)H(g)H(g) is dual to H(1/g)H(1/g)H(1/g). A similar phrasing can be posed for 2D Ising because 1D transverse Field Ising is dual to 2D Ising. It can be easily shown via transfer matrix methods (cf. Kogut RMP.) Isn't it confusing that for large ggg, H(g)H(g)H(g) is in the paramagnetic phase and hence has one ground state, while H(1/g)H(1/g)H(1/g) is in the ferromagnetic phase, undergoes SSB and has two ground states? We study the Kramers-Wannier duality more carefully, on a closed chain. It is important to notice that the closed chain is not defined trivially here. We consider a length NNN ring by gluing together spins at position 000 and NNN via imposing bounday conditions. We can either phrase the boundary conditions in operator language or state language. But either way, there are constraints that the BCs must satisfy: they must preserve the operator algebra (which is automatic if we can find a state interpretation) and they must respect the Z2\mathbb{Z}_2Z2​ symmetry of Ising model, P=∏i=0N−1σixP=\prod_{i=0}^{N-1}\sigma_i^xP=∏i=0N−1​σix​.

Here are the two boundary conditions:

  1. Periodic(P). That is, σ0a=σNa\sigma_0^a=\sigma_N^aσ0a​=σNa​, which means that ∣0⟩0=∣0⟩N\ket{0}_0=\ket{0}_N∣0⟩0​=∣0⟩N​, ∣1⟩0=∣1⟩N\ket{1}_0=\ket{1}_N∣1⟩0​=∣1⟩N​.

  2. Anti-Periodic(AP). That is, σ0z=−σNz,σ0x=σNx,σ0y=−σNy\sigma^z_0=-\sigma^z_N,\sigma^x_0=\sigma^x_N,\sigma^y_0=-\sigma^y_Nσ0z​=−σNz​,σ0x​=σNx​,σ0y​=−σNy​, i.e. ∣0⟩0=∣1⟩N\ket{0}_0=\ket{1}_N∣0⟩0​=∣1⟩N​, ∣1⟩0=∣0⟩N\ket{1}_0=\ket{0}_N∣1⟩0​=∣0⟩N​.

Now we consider the Kramers-Wannier Duality. We define τ\tauτ variables on the bonds between sites:

τi+1/2x=σizσi+1z,τi−1/2zτi+1/2z=σix\tau^x_{i+1/2}=\sigma^z_i\sigma^z_{i+1},\tau^z_{i-1/2}\tau^z_{i+1/2}=\sigma^x_iτi+1/2x​=σiz​σi+1z​,τi−1/2z​τi+1/2z​=σix​

And thus if we consider the parity symmetry in the dual model with τ\tauτ variables, we have

P=∏i=0N−1τi+1/2x=∏i=0N−1σizσi+1z=σ0zσNz=±1P=\prod_{i=0}^{N-1}\tau^x_{i+1/2}=\prod_{i=0}^{N-1}\sigma^z_i\sigma^z_{i+1} = \sigma^z_0\sigma^z_N = \pm 1P=i=0∏N−1​τi+1/2x​=i=0∏N−1​σiz​σi+1z​=σ0z​σNz​=±1

Hence we observe that the parity symmetry charge measures the different boundary conditions in the dual picture.

Consider the large ggg case for the most intuitive periodic boundary condition. In σ\sigmaσ variables, there is only one ground state. In τ\tauτ variables, the corresponding ground state is unique: ∣GS⟩=12(∣0…0⟩+∣1…1⟩\ket{GS}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{0\dots 0 }+\ket{1\dots 1}∣GS⟩=2​1​(∣0…0⟩+∣1…1⟩, because the other cat state has parity −1-1−1. We have thus resolved the paradox.

This subtlety can be further considered for the Jordan-Wigner transformation. In that case, partition functions are completely different for free fermions in 1+1d under different boundary conditions (cf. P. Ginsparg, Applied CFT.)
